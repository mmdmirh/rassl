Our Research: Modelling and Optimizing Complex Systems

At the Resource Allocation and Stochastic Systems Lab (RASSL), we tackle fundamental challenges in the design and operation of modern computing and service systems. Our work is grounded in rigorous mathematical principles and aimed at delivering practical solutions for a more efficient and reliable world. Click on each pillar below to learn more.

Stochastic Modelling

The world is filled with randomnessâ€”from the arrival of user requests at a web server to the processing time of a complex computation. Stochastic modelling is the language we use to describe and understand these unpredictable systems. We develop sophisticated mathematical models that capture the inherent uncertainty in system behaviour, providing a foundation for robust analysis and prediction. This approach allows us to move beyond simple averages and account for the full range of possible outcomes, ensuring our solutions are resilient in real-world conditions.

Scheduling

At its heart, scheduling is about making intelligent decisions: which task should run on which processor, and when? In an era of massive parallel systems and cloud computing, effective scheduling is the key to unlocking performance. Our research focuses on designing and analyzing novel scheduling algorithms that optimize for various objectives, such as minimizing response time, maximizing throughput, ensuring fairness among users, and reducing energy consumption. We explore dynamic policies that can adapt in real-time to changing workloads and system states.

Performance Evaluation

How do we know if a system is working well? Performance evaluation provides the answer. Using a combination of mathematical analysis, simulation, and real-world measurement, we develop methodologies to quantify the efficiency, scalability, and reliability of complex systems. Our work aims to identify performance bottlenecks, compare competing system designs, and predict how a system will behave under future growth. This rigorous, quantitative approach ensures that our proposed improvements are not just theoretical but deliver measurable benefits.

Queueing Theory

From data packets traversing the internet to jobs waiting in a cloud computing cluster, waiting linesâ€”or queuesâ€”are everywhere. Queueing theory is the powerful mathematical toolset for analyzing these phenomena. RASSL applies and extends queueing theory to model system delays, predict wait times, and determine optimal resource capacity. By understanding the dynamics of queues, we can design systems that are both highly responsive to users and cost-effective to operate, preventing over-provisioning while avoiding unacceptable delays.

Data Center Control Systems

Modern data centers are the engines of the digital economy, but they are immensely complex ecosystems that consume vast amounts of energy. This research area integrates all of our core expertise to address the unique challenges of data center management. Our projects focus on creating intelligent control systems that can dynamically manage workloads and resources. This includes developing algorithms for load balancing to prevent server overload, thermal-aware scheduling to manage heat and reduce cooling costs, and energy-proportional computing to minimize the environmental footprint of cloud services. Our goal is to create the blueprint for the sustainable, autonomous, and high-performance data centers of the future.
